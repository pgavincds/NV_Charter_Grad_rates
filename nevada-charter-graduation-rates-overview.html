Overview

Nevada Charter Graduation Rates

This project estimates Nevada charter-sector 4-year graduation rates from public school-level records, while preserving official statewide and district comparison rates exactly as Nevada reported them.

The goal is not to replace Nevada's official files. It is to make the charter sector legible across time, especially where school openings, sponsorship changes, tiny cohorts, and alternative-school treatment make the public record harder to read.

Purpose

Why This Exists

Nevada publishes official authorizer and district graduation rates, but those public files do not always give a clean charter-sector breakout by school model, school mission, or historical authorizer. This project builds that layer transparently from school-year evidence.

The public artifact is strongest where the state published direct school-level ACGR rows and where grade-by-grade enrollment files confirm which schools actually had 12th graders in a given year.
Scope

What The Files Separate

  • Official statewide and district rows from NDE annual ACGR releases
  • Estimated SPCSA, Clark, and Washoe charter slices
  • SPCSA schools sorted into clearer type-based groups such as brick and mortar, virtual, and alternative rather than left in one blended average
  • The school-level drilldowns let readers see the specific campuses inside those groups, including Nevada Connections, Nevada Virtual, Leadership Academy, and Beacon
  • Historical ASD rows kept under ASD for ASD years
How To Read It

What Counts As Official And What Counts As Estimated

The statewide, district, and authorizer totals shown here are official public numbers taken from Nevada's annual ACGR releases. The charter slices are estimated from school-level public rows and should be read as transparent reconstructions rather than sponsor-issued subgroup totals.

In practice, that means a row like SPCSA official public is Nevada's published sponsor total, while a row like SPCSA adjusted charter-only or SPCSA brick and mortar is a school-level estimate built from counted campuses in that class year.
Method

Interpretation Rules

  • Historical authorizer stays tied to the reporting year.
  • Historical NDE sponsor reporting appears to have mixed some non-charter entities into SPCSA public totals, so the project keeps the official SPCSA line as published but also shows a separate adjusted charter-only SPCSA estimate.
  • District option schools are excluded if they were not legally charter schools.
  • Suppressed top-coded rows use a 95.0 to 100.0 interval with midpoint 97.5.
  • Suppressed bottom-coded rows use a 0.0 to 5.0 interval with midpoint 2.5.
  • When a school had no 12th grade and no contrary ACGR row, that school-year is excluded rather than held.
  • Democracy Prep is treated as an Achievement School District school in 2017-18 and 2018-19.
  • `Davidson` and `Independence` are excluded from the SPCSA charter-only estimate even where historical official SPCSA totals may still deserve a caveat.
  • The separate virtual and alternative columns are there to show how different policy and operating environments shape outcomes, not just to create drilldown menus.

Use

Use Cases

  • Public policy writing about Nevada charter performance trends
  • Authorizer-by-authorizer historical comparison
  • Model-specific comparisons such as virtual, alternative, and brick-and-mortar
  • External replication or challenge of school-year inclusion decisions
Takeaways

What Stands Out

The early years required the most cleanup. A large share of apparent legacy gaps turned out not to be missing graduation data at all, but schools that had not yet reached 12th grade.
The separate SPCSA adjusted charter-only line is most useful in the earlier years. By roughly 2018-19 forward, the public files look materially cleaner, so later gaps between the official sponsor total and the screened charter-only estimate should be read more cautiously.
Alternative and transition cases matter. Beacon is clearer when separated from general brick-and-mortar trends, and Democracy Prep reads more honestly when its ASD-era years remain attached to ASD rather than being back-cast into SPCSA.
Tiny programs can distort impressions if left unexamined. Several early Nevada State High School campus rows were better treated as not yet operating in the graduation universe rather than as unresolved missing data.
The statewide online picture is easier to read when the schools are separated. Nevada Connections and Nevada Virtual now appear as their own lines, and Leadership Academy is also shown separately even though the working panel still codes it as alternative rather than statewide online.
The model-specific breakouts matter substantively, not cosmetically. The alternative and virtual slices make it easier to see how structure, accountability rules, and operator behavior influenced outcomes over time instead of hiding those differences inside one blended charter average.
