Nevada Charter Quality Seats

Did Nevada charter growth translate into more high-quality seats?

An ongoing high-level dashboard tracking whether Nevada charter growth is translating into more students in stronger schools.

Nevada's School Performance Framework assigns schools an annual rating from 1 star to 5 stars. In this dashboard, 4- and 5-star schools are treated as higher-rated/high-quality schools, 3-star schools are treated as a middle or mixed-performance category, and 1- and 2-star schools are treated as lower-rated schools. A 3-star rating can reflect strength on either growth or proficiency, but it is not the same as sustained strength across both.

Main finding: The available data indicate that Nevada's charter sector now has both more high-quality seats and a larger share of charter students enrolled in high-quality schools than at any prior point in the available data series.

That combination matters. More 4- and 5-star seats alone could simply mean the sector got bigger. A higher high-quality share alone could reflect shrinkage or closure of weaker schools. But both happening together is stronger evidence of portfolio improvement: the sector grew, and more of that enrollment was concentrated in schools rated at the higher end of Nevada's performance framework.

This dashboard presents an ongoing high-level view of a portfolio-management question Nevada leaders and policymakers were already asking in 2014: as the charter sector grew, were more students actually being served in stronger schools?

CREDO's 2013 national charter study landed hard in Nevada. It prompted defensiveness and excuse-making, but it also prompted necessary soul-searching about charter quality, authorizing, closure, replication, and whether sector growth was actually producing better opportunities for students. At the time, Nevada was not merely below average; it was the state other troubled charter sectors were relieved to be compared against.

During that period, Pedro Martinez, then serving as Superintendent in Residence at the Nevada Department of Education under State Superintendent Dale Erquiaga during Governor Brian Sandoval's administration, challenged Nevada leaders and policymakers to move beyond compliance reporting and use data to show what was working, where progress was real, and where serious work remained.

CREDO's 2023 national study later singled out Nevada for the largest increase in charter-school academic growth in both reading and math among states appearing in both the 2013 and 2023 studies. Nevada did not move from worst in the nation to best in the nation. But it did move from the bottom of the national charter-performance table to performance that was not statistically different from comparable traditional public school peers.

That improvement was real, but it was also a lagging indicator. The 2023 CREDO report used data from 2014-15 through 2018-19, meaning it captured the early results of Nevada's post-2013 quality and authorizing work, not the later wave of charter enrollment growth. Much of the more recent increase in charter seats falls outside that study window and may never be captured in a comparable national CREDO update.

CREDO was not the only pressure point. Another major critique of the Nevada charter sector involved graduation performance. Based on 2013-14 graduation-rate data, the SPCSA was the lowest-performing local education agency in Nevada. With state testing disrupted during the transition to a new assessment system, graduation rates became an especially important immediate indicator for Nevada leaders and policymakers. That is why this quality-seats dashboard should be read alongside the weighted graduation-rate companion: one page asks whether more students were being served in stronger schools, while the other asks whether sector-level graduation-rate claims reflect students rather than schools.

A recent BullshitED post restated the same challenge in current language: charter-sector reporting should not treat gross seats added as equivalent to sector strength. The relevant question is whether the portfolio gained net, durable, quality-weighted capacity.

This dashboard applies that authorizing and portfolio-management discipline to Nevada's public NSPF and enrollment data. The point is not simply to count schools opened or seats added. It is to ask whether Nevada expanded access to stronger schools, where that claim is supported, and where the evidence still requires caution.

Technical notes and limitations

Technical notes and limitations: This dashboard shows recovered legacy years directly in the charts so the reader can see the historical arc. The earliest years are more directional than the later workbook-based years, but they are included because they help explain the sector's trajectory rather than making the charter sector appear suddenly in the middle of the timeline.

The first charts show stacked sectorwide seat counts by Nevada NSPF star rating over time, then sector mix, and then total enrollment context. For consistency across authorizers, school bands are standardized statewide as .1 = ES, .2 = MS, and .3 = HS.

  • Visible chart years: 2012-13 to 2024-25
  • Legacy 2012-13 to 2014-15 shown directionally
  • 2015-16 omitted from chart sequence
  • 2021-22 omitted from chart sequence
  • Not Rated schools are excluded from the main rating charts
  • Statewide suffix convention: .1 = ES, .2 = MS, .3 = HS
  • Spillover grades are excluded rather than reassigned
  • Manual-review bucket preserved for transparency
  • 2012-13 through 2014-15 come from legacy state-charter accountability recoveries rather than the later modern workbook pipeline
  • For multi-band legacy schools, ES/MS/HS seat counts are estimated from the nearest later validation-day band shares because the older PDFs publish school totals but not exact band-level seat counts
  • 2015-16 is still best understood as a transition year, but it is retained in the download files rather than shown in the visible chart sequence
